Adjustable Posture-Correction Neck Brace

Designed and prototyped a passive, mechanical neck brace to limit excessive forward neck flexion and encourage healthier posture

We developed a shoulder-supported chin brace that physically prevents harmful downward head tilt, rather than relying on behavioral feedback. The project required building a cardboard looks-like prototype to validate fit and aesthetics, developing two iterations of a 3D-printed works-like prototype with adjustable chin width, chin height, and shoulder length, testing the device on our own team, and benchmarking the result against existing posture-correction products on the market.

Project Partners: Sujal Bobba, Andrew Darmitzel, Aaditya Shivadey, Audrey Young

Background

Forward neck flexion, commonly caused by prolonged slouching or "tech neck," is a widespread posture problem with no fully satisfying mechanical solution on the market. Existing products generally fall into two categories: soft shoulder and back braces that emphasize comfort and wearability but create a reliance that causes muscle atrophy, and smart electronic devices, that detect slouching and provide vibration feedback that are unreliable at actually preventing posture-related neck strain.

We set out to design a device with a passive, physical-intervention philosophy, but with meaningfully more adjustability for users with varying bodily dimensions, while keeping the device low-cost and quick to put on.

Design Process

Interviews

Before prototyping, we interviewed medical professionals as well as potential users of posture-correction devices. The medical professional interviews were especially formative: they helped establish the initial need for this kind of device and surfaced a key design constraint, that the brace had to avoid causing muscle atrophy. This pushed us toward a design that acts as a behavioral corrector, intervening only when the user tilts their head beyond a target threshold, rather than a resting brace that passively supports the head and could let the neck muscles weaken from disuse. Interviews with potential users added two more practical requirements: something low-cost with minimal time required to put the device on, and adjustability, particularly in chin width and height, to fit individual neck and jaw dimensions.

We then came up with dozens of potential solutions, and created a Pugh chart to compare our three best solutions. This led us to settle on the elastic chin support design:

Our prototyping process consisted of two main stages: a looks-like prototype, followed by a works-like prototype. The looks-like prototype assessed fit and aesthetics. Critical dimensions, the length and width of the chin support, the height of the chin support relative to the shoulder, and the length of the shoulder support, were chosen by researching the 5th–95th percentile range of human dimensions and selecting a single representative value. Cardboard was used for the structure: a team member's chin and shoulder were traced, cut out, and fashioned into a brace, with a felt neck strap adhered to the back using duct tape.

Testing on team members showed the chin support was too wide to fit properly, and its shape prevented users from tilting their heads side to side, so it needed to be made smaller and moved closer to the shoulders. The shoulder support's height and shape fit users as intended, and the felt neck strap anchored the brace well, though its fixed length couldn't fit every team member. Cardboard represented the shape and size well but couldn't provide the rigidity needed for a practical prototype, so we moved to PLA 3D printing for greater strength, rigidity, and design flexibility, and planned to add adjustability for chin support height, the angle of the chin support relative to the shoulder support, and chin width in future iterations.

Works-Like Prototype: Initial Iteration

The initial works-like prototype required three key features: a chin support that contacts the user's chin upon excessive neck tilt, a shoulder piece providing a base support, and a ratcheting mechanism allowing the angle between the chin and shoulder supports to be adjusted. A gear-and-pawl mechanism was integrated at the chin-to-shoulder joint for that angular adjustment. The chin piece's top was kept flat so it wouldn't contact the user's chin with a protruding edge, while the shoulder piece was curved to match the upper shoulders. Parts were dimensioned from one team member's chin and neck measurements (chin-to-jaw, jaw-to-neck, and neck-to-upper-chest distances) and 3D printed on a Bambu X1 Carbon and a Prusa MK3S.

Assessing this prototype, we determined several changes were needed: the chin support width needed to be made adjustable, and its overall height needed to be greatly reduced. An elastic mechanism needed to be added to the chin support to provide a variable, spring-like force against the user's chin, rather than a rigid stop. The shoulder support needed to be lengthened and made adjustable, and the ratcheting mechanism's tolerances needed to be tightened slightly for a more solid fit.

Works-Like Prototype: Final Iteration

The final works-like prototype addressed all of the above changes. The chin support's height was reduced by 3 cm and split into two pieces connected by a pin joint and an elastic band in tension; when force is applied to the chin support, it pivots about the joint and stretches the elastic, creating a spring-like force against the user's chin. An internal rack-and-pawl mechanism along the chin section, connecting both sides, allowed the chin width to be adjusted by a total of 3 cm by sliding the two halves apart or together. The shoulder support was lengthened by 4 cm and split into two parts that slide along each other and fasten with a set screw, giving 3 cm of total length adjustability. The neck strap material was also changed from felt to Velcro to allow greater adjustability for varying neck dimensions.

Manufacturing

All works-like prototype components were 3D printed in PLA on a Bambu X1 Carbon and a Prusa MK3S, chosen over the cardboard and felt of the looks-like prototype for greater strength, rigidity, and design flexibility. We aimed to minimize unnecessary hardware, relying on a printed rack-and-pawl mechanism, a single pin joint, an elastic band, set screws, and Velcro, in order to keep the device low-cost and built from accessible materials. This let us move through three full prototype iterations, looks-like, initial works-like, and final works-like, fitted and tested on the same subject within the project timeline.

Results & Testing

User tests consisted of each team member, each with varying neck and chin dimensions, donning the device and adjusting it to their body. Although our testing sample was limited to our own team, the variation in our anthropometric dimensions gave a reasonable initial approximation of what other users would experience. Testing focused on the most heavily weighted sections of our Pugh chart: the device's effectiveness in limiting forward neck flexion, comfort during normal use, and ease of donning and doffing. Each team member verbally described how the device did or didn't meet these requirements, and we used those descriptions, along with our own experience building the prototype, for our overall assessment.

Effectiveness: the chin-stop design did effectively prevent users from tilting their heads beyond the target threshold, validating our passive, chin-and-shoulder-based approach.

Ease of use: donning and doffing the device was manageable, but making adjustments while wearing it was difficult. The angular adjustment mechanism was hard for users to align since they couldn't visually inspect its position.

Comfort: the device was generally comfortable, except that the chin support width was too small, causing discomfort for some users.

Manufacturability: all parts could be 3D printed and joined with standard fasteners, but some components were fragile and, despite our force calcs, were prone to breaking. This pointed to a need for stronger materials or more robust geometries.

Complexity: the number of prototypes required to reach our current design point indicates a moderately high level of complexity. Achieving proper integration between the functional mechanisms and the upper body proved more challenging than we initially expected.

Based on this assessment, the report identified several changes for a future version: the default chin support width should be significantly increased so the device sits around the neck rather than in front of it, and the overall shoulder support length should be decreased so the brace rests on the upper part of the shoulders rather than the front of the shoulders and upper chest. The elastic within the chin support should be changed to a stiffer material, such as a steel torsion spring, to provide a greater and more affirmative force against tilting. The neck strap's mounting slot should be enlarged to allow easier adjustment and reduce the likelihood of material failure, and future versions should use more reinforced geometries and resilient materials overall, since multiple components fractured during prototyping.

Comparison to Existing Solutions

The Latestaa Neck Brace was the only commercial product we identified with a similar design to ours, sharing the same fundamental goal of preventing excessive downward head tilt using a passive chin rest mounted to the upper body. The key difference was adjustability: the Latestaa brace relies on fixed geometry, with its cloth neck strap as the only adjustable feature, while our design incorporates adjustability across chin width, angle, and shoulder support length, in addition to an adjustable neck strap. Our device is also designed as a behavioral corrector that only intervenes once the user's head tilts past a target threshold, rather than a resting brace that continuously supports the head, a distinction informed directly by our medical professional interviews and their concern about muscle atrophy. Latestaa's device does, however, successfully integrate silicone padding and injection-molded plastic, giving it greater comfort and durability than our prototype's cloth padding and 3D-printed PLA construction.

Lessons Learned & Next Steps

The biggest challenge of the project was meeting our original 5th–95th percentile sizing goal: across iterations, our chin support kept coming out too tall and not wide enough for users outside our test subject's specific dimensions. We addressed this within our time and budget constraints by reframing the goal from a universal fit to a user-specific customization approach, designing and iterating tightly around one team member rather than the full range. The device's excessive height and narrow chin support width limit how universally it can fit and may affect comfort for users outside that specific range; the report's final testing also noted that the brace tended to shift or rotate during use, suggesting the current support length and contact surfaces don't yet provide adequate stability.

Throughout the project, we applied a recurring design cycle, framing and reframing the problem, imagining and designing possible solutions, building and experimenting with prototypes, and observing and noting what did and didn't work, repeating that loop on the same test subject across the looks-like prototype and the two works-like iterations. What was most useful from the class in shaping the project was learning to design against the 5th–95th percentile range for a target market, the structured design process itself, and the practice of conducting user interviews before committing to a design direction.

Working through this project shifted our perspective on designing for the human body: it's genuinely difficult to fit a single adjustable mechanical design to a wide range of body dimensions, and producing multiple prototypes, testing them, and iterating quickly was what let us converge on a design that actually worked for our test subject. If time and money were not constraints, our next step would be to redesign and reprint the prototype with updated dimensions, reducing the chin support height and increasing its width, and to either use 3D printers capable of multiple materials to build a sleeker integrated elastic element into the chin support, or replace the elastic band with torsion springs for a stiffer, more reliable response.
