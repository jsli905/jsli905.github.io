Gimballed Cupholder

Created a gimballed cupholder for scooters/bikes on UC Berkeley's bumpy and hilly roads.

This project involved the design and manufacturing of a functional cupholder system, with a strong emphasis on design for manufacturability (DFM), tolerance control, and proper application of Geometric Dimensioning and Tolerancing (GD&T)

Design Considerations

The design prioritized usability, adaptability, and robustness to ensure the cupholder performs reliably across a range of real-world conditions.

Adjustability

  • Designed to accommodate a range of cup diameters and geometries
  • Incorporated compliant or adjustable features to handle size variation
  • Avoided over-constraining the cup to prevent binding or instability

Ease of Use

  • Single-handed operation for insertion and removal
  • Minimal force required for adjustment
  • Clear and intuitive interaction (no precise alignment required)

Stability & Retention

  • Geometry designed to prevent tipping during motion
  • Maintains secure contact without excessive clamping force
  • Accounts for dynamic loads (e.g., vehicle motion)

Material Selection

Material selection was driven by a balance between mechanical performance, manufacturability, and cost.

Key Requirements:
  • Sufficient stiffness to maintain shape under load
  • Durability under repeated use and cyclic loading
  • Resistance to environmental conditions (temperature, moisture)
  • Compatibility with selected manufacturing process

Materials Used

  • Clamp: PLA
  • Outer Ring: 1/2” Aluminum, 1/8” Ball Bearings (quantity: 2)
  • Middle Ring: 1/2” Aluminum, 1/8” Ball Bearings (quantity: 4)
  • Cup Holder: Markforge X7 Nylon, 1/2” long 1/4”-20 bolts (quantity: 3), 1/4”-20 nuts (quantity: 3), washers (quantity: 3)

Manufacturing Methods

  • Clamp: 3D printed using a Bambu Lab P1S FDM 3D printer
  • Outer Ring: Outer ring and T key feature were water jetted as separate parts. The bearing holes were milled.
  • Middle Ring: Main shape was water jetted. The bearing holes were milled.
  • Cup Holder: 3D printed using the Markforged X7 Carbon Fiber FDM 3D printer

The final material selection reflects a compromise between structural integrity and functional flexibility, particularly for adjustable components.

Design for Manufacturing (DFM)

The design was developed to ensure compatibility with common manufacturing processes while minimizing cost and complexity.

  • Simplified geometry to reduce machining operations
  • Minimized tight tolerances where not functionally required
  • Avoided unnecessary undercuts and complex features
  • Designed for ease of assembly and repeatability

Tolerancing Strategy

A critical aspect of the design was defining tolerances that balance functionality, manufacturability, and cost.

Key Considerations:
  • Loose tolerances applied to non-critical features
  • Tight tolerances reserved for mating and alignment surfaces
  • Stack-up analysis performed to ensure proper fit
  • Consideration of manufacturing variability

This approach reduced manufacturing difficulty while ensuring reliable assembly and performance.

Engineering Drawings

GD&T was used to clearly communicate design intent and ensure consistent part quality.

Results & Design Evaluation

The final design successfully met key functional and manufacturing objectives, including secure cup retention, adaptability to varying cup sizes, and compatibility with standard manufacturing processes. The implementation of GD&T improved dimensional clarity and ensured consistent assembly performance.

However, several real-world considerations influenced performance beyond the idealized design. Manufacturing variability, material compliance, and tolerance stack-up introduced deviations that affected fit and stability. These factors highlighted the importance of balancing precision with manufacturability.

Key Observations:
  • Adjustable geometry improved usability but introduced sensitivity to tolerance stack-up
  • Material flexibility aided adaptability but reduced structural rigidity under load
  • Critical dimensions required tighter tolerances to maintain alignment and stability
  • GD&T framework significantly improved communication of design intent

Overall, the project demonstrated the trade-offs between design flexibility, structural performance, and manufacturing constraints, reinforcing the importance of integrated engineering design.
