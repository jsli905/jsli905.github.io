Pedal Tray Assembly

Designed and iterated on the B23 pedal tray assembly, including the brake pedal, throttle pedal, return mechanism, linear potentiometer mounting, pedal cups, and heel rest.

The B23 pedal tray was a ground-up redesign focused on weight reduction, improved driver ergonomics, and packaging of new sensor hardware. The design went through CDR, DDR, and FDR review cycles, followed by extensive post-FDR iteration to resolve sensor accuracy and structural issues discovered during testing.

Overview

The B23 pedal tray assembly introduced several significant changes over B22: a new single-wall throttle pedal mounting design, a redesigned brake pedal with an I-beam cross section and upgraded material, a simplified torsion spring return mechanism, new linear potentiometer mounting locations, and entirely new driver ergonomic features in the form of 3D printed carbon-fiber-reinforced pedal cups and a heel rest. The assembly also provides 3 inches of fore-aft pedal adjustability at 1-inch increments to accommodate different driver sizes.

Post-FDR testing revealed several issues with the potentiometer mounting and throttle pedal torsional stiffness that required multiple rounds of redesign and reprinting before the system met functional requirements.

Pedal Tray

Project Owner: Donald Le.
Contributions: Contributed on design and engineering trade studies + simulation setup and validation. Manufactured and machinined pedal tray.

The pedal tray is the structural backbone of the assembly, mounting the brake pedal, master cylinders, throttle pedal, and all associated hardware to the chassis floor. It is CNC milled from 6061-T6 aluminum.

Design Changes

The most significant structural change was switching the throttle pedal side of the tray to a single-wall design, reducing weight from 0.72 lbs to 0.63 lbs while also simplifying assembly. The brake pedal side geometry was largely carried over from B22. The throttle side walls were also made thinner overall. A key packaging improvement was that the potentiometer mounts were designed to double as holders for the torsion springs, reducing part count. Pedal cup and heel rest mounting tabs were added, and the tray was given standoff holes to allow the heel rest distance to be adjusted.

FEA Simulation

The tray was simulated in both SolidWorks and Ansys under a 450 lb brake pedal input, with pin connectors at the master cylinder and brake pedal interfaces, a rigid floor constraint at the top face, bonded dummy MCs and pedal bodies, and bolted tab connectors with 1850 lbf preload. Results across both simulation environments were consistent: max stress of approximately 210 MPa (30,460 psi) and a factor of safety of ~1.2.

Brake Pedal

Project Owner: Donald Le.
Contributions: Collaborated extensively on pedal design and iteration, assisted with simulation setup and validation, conducted testing and analysis of the previous B22 pedal design, and contributed to machined the final assembly.

Design

The overall geometry of the brake pedal was retained from B22, but several targeted changes were made to improve structural performance, reduce weight, and improve compatibility with the new tray design. The pedal is machined from 7050-T6351 aluminum, upgraded from 6061-T6, to allow for more aggressive material removal while maintaining a higher factor of safety. An I-beam cross section was incorporated into the shank to reduce deflection under load while also reducing weight, bringing the total from 0.70 lbs to 0.68 lbs. The pedal height was increased by 0.5 inches to improve leverage and better align with the master cylinder actuation geometry. Stainless steel ball bearings replaced the previous bronze bushings for smoother pivot actuation, and mounting points for the pedal cup were added to the sides of the pedal face.

FEA Simulation

The brake pedal was simulated under a 450 lb face load with a frictionless support and remote displacement constraint at the pivot. The final Ansys simulation produced a max von Mises stress of 210 MPa (iso-clipped at 100 MPa to visualize stress distribution) and a max displacement of 2.9 mm. An earlier SolidWorks simulation produced consistent results at 30,312.9 psi max stress and 0.11 inches max displacement, confirming the design was adequate before switching simulation environments.

Physical Testing

A destructive test was conducted on the previous-generation (B20) brake pedal to determine whether it had been underbuilt or overbuilt, informing the design margin for B23. The test fixture used two high-carbon steel square stock pieces to simulate the pedal at maximum deflection angle with pistons restricting further travel. The assembly was oriented on its side to allow an Instron load frame to apply force directly to the pedal face. Results from this test were used to calibrate simulation assumptions and set the target FOS for the B23 design.

Throttle Pedal

Design

The throttle pedal retained its two-waterjet-piece parallel plate construction from B22, using two 0.16-inch 6061-T6 aluminum plates with aluminum spacers. Smaller bronze bushings were used to accommodate a smaller 5/16" shoulder bolt. The pedal weighs 0.27 lbs. The major changes relative to B22 were in the linear potentiometer mounting locations — the new single-wall tray design required moving the potentiometers, which enabled material reduction on the tray while also spacing the sensors further apart for better independent readings. Custom-made torsion springs were introduced to allow return force to be customized based on individual driver feedback, replacing the off-the-shelf springs used previously.

FEA Simulation

The throttle pedal was simulated under a 50 lb face load with a frictionless support at the hard stop and a remote displacement constraint at the pivot. Both SolidWorks and Ansys simulations were run. SolidWorks results: max stress 18,110 psi, max displacement 0.085 inches. Ansys results: max stress 116 MPa (1.16 MPa is a typo in the slide — the stress contour scale shows 1.16 × 10⁸ Pa), max displacement 2.1 mm. The throttle pedal is lightly loaded relative to the brake pedal, and the simulation confirmed substantial margin.

Return Mechanism

The B22 return mechanism used a two-wall bracket design that added weight and made assembly more difficult. The B23 design simplified this to a single-wall configuration, reducing both weight and part count while maintaining the same torsion spring architecture. Magnets are not used on the throttle return — the torsion springs provide all return force. The potentiometer mounts on the tray were designed to act as integrated spring holders, eliminating separate retaining hardware.

Linear Potentiometers

Both the throttle and brake pedals use linear potentiometers to measure pedal position. The mounting locations were redesigned for B23 to reduce material needed on the pedal tray and move the two throttle potentiometers further apart for more independent position readings. The brake pedal position sensor was added as a new feature on B23, providing direct brake pedal travel data to the DAQ system.

The potentiometer mounts were designed to also serve as torsion spring holders, consolidating two functions into a single part and simplifying the assembly. Smaller potentiometers were targeted for future iterations to allow improved pedal geometry and further weight reduction.

Pedal Cups and Heel Rest

The B23 pedal tray introduced driver ergonomic contact surfaces for the first time: carbon-fiber-reinforced 3D printed pedal cups and a heel rest. Both were printed on a MarkForged printer using Onyx (carbon-fiber-filled nylon) with continuous carbon fiber reinforcement for stiffness. Geometry and dimensions were based directly on driver feedback gathered during testing.

Pedal Cups

The pedal cups mount to the sides of the brake pedal face rather than the face itself, an improvement over the B22 mounting approach that reduced stress concentrations on the pedal. This side-mounting strategy also made the cups easier to install and remove independently of the pedal.

Heel Rest

The heel rest mounts to tabs on the tray floor using female threaded round standoffs and stainless steel flat head screws, with standoff height used to adjust the heel rest distance from the tray. The part uses reinforcing flanges on the back face to increase the moment area and overall stiffness, an open shell in the center to reduce weight, and side walls to restrict lateral driver foot movement. An inverted T-shaped cutout at the base allows the part to slide over the mounting tabs for installation.

Testing Issues and Iterations

The pedal tray went through several significant redesign cycles after testing began as issues were discovered during on-car testing. These are documented here both for completeness and because they represent the bulk of the real engineering problem-solving work on this subsystem.

Sensor Dynamic Range Issues

After switching to larger linear potentiometers, the throttle pedal travel was insufficient to reach the required dynamic range of the sensors needed for accurate throttle position readings. The sensors were physically too long for the available pedal stroke, meaning the output voltage swing across the full pedal travel was too small to be reliably interpreted by the DAQ system.

Shorter linear potentiometers were sourced to match the available pedal stroke, but this required a full redesign of the potentiometer mounting on the pedal tray to accommodate the new sensor geometry. The tray mounting features were reprinted and the sensor mounting brackets were updated accordingly.

Torsional Stiffness and Tracking Faults

During testing, the throttle pedal exhibited insufficient torsional stiffness. Under driver input, the pedal would flex and twist, causing the two linear potentiometers to read inconsistent positions relative to each other. The DAQ system uses both potentiometer readings to verify throttle position and detect sensor faults — when the readings diverged beyond the tolerance threshold due to physical flex rather than a true sensor failure, tracking faults were triggered and throttle inputs could not be interpreted correctly.

The fix was to move both throttle potentiometers to the same side of the pedal, mounted directly adjacent to each other. With both sensors at the same location, flex-induced divergence between the two readings was eliminated and the tracking faults were resolved. This was validated on-car after the modification.

A separate delamination issue was identified on the brake pedal cup mounting area. The pedal side in that region was reprinted with additional reinforcement geometry and increased material thickness to prevent the layers from separating under repeated pedal load cycles.

Future Work

Several improvements were identified for future iterations but not implemented on B23:

The linear potentiometer length constraint ultimately points to a need to redesign the pedals themselves, not just the tray mounting, to accommodate shorter sensors with the correct stroke geometry from the start. The current approach of adapting the mounting around available sensors creates packaging compromises that cascade into structural and accuracy issues.

Additional items flagged for future designs include adding a hardline constraint system on the tray floor to secure brake lines more rigidly, and evaluating a failsafe mechanism for loss of hydraulic pressure in either the front or rear circuit to ensure the over-travel switch is not inadvertently triggered under a partial system failure.
