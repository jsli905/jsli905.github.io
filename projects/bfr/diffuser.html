Undertray and Diffuser Aerodynamics

Used CFD simulation to iterate on B23's diffuser geometry and side wing strakes as preliminary design work for B24, optimizing downforce contribution within packaging constraints.

As part of the aerodynamics subteam, I set up and ran CFD simulations to iterate on the undertray diffuser and side wing geometry inherited from B23, exploring dimensional changes to inform the B24 design. The base diffuser profile and side wing concept were developed by previous team members; my work focused on simulating and testing variations on this existing platform.

Diffuser Profile

The B23 diffuser uses a curved Benzing D profile, a departure from the flat diffusers used on B18 and B19. Curved diffusers were avoided in those years due to their more complex flow characteristics, but per Zhang's research on ground effect aerodynamics, these complex flow features — including vortex formation and separated flow regions — are expected and even necessary for an optimal diffuser, provided the flow remains attached and vortices stay stable rather than breaking down asymmetrically.

Simulation of the B18 diffuser geometry showed reversed flow within the diffuser, but with a strong, stable vortex and attached flow along the upper surface — consistent with the "OK" surface flow pattern identified in Zhang's reference study, as opposed to the vortex reversal and symmetry breakdown seen in poorly performing diffuser profiles. Turbulence in the middle of the diffuser was also expected and consistent with this regime. This analysis framed the design space I worked within when iterating on B23's geometry for B24.

Side Undertrays with Integrated Sidewings

The B23 side undertray sections use a Benzing D diffuser profile on the inboard side, paired with an outboard profile incorporating a side wing array that functions similarly to a diffuser pump, accelerating flow to support the main diffuser's pressure recovery. Each side wing is a two-element S1223 airfoil with integrated endplate strakes to manage spanwise flow and reduce tip losses. This base design was developed by previous team members.

Building on this platform, I set up CFD cases to iterate on diffuser dimensions — testing increased diffuser width and length to explore how far downforce could be pushed within the geometric envelope set by the chassis and suspension components. I also tested different strake widths on the side wing endplates to evaluate their effect on downforce and flow attachment, as preliminary direction-setting for the B24 design.

The side undertray and wing assemblies are manufactured from 7-ply carbon fiber with two layers of Rohacell core for additional rigidity.

Undertray, Sidewings, and Sidepod Integration

The undertray inlet uses a diverging cross-section, increasing from 0.7× to 1× the radiator frontal area to smoothly accelerate and condition flow before it reaches the radiator. The inlet is also asymmetric in shape, routing air parallel to the radiator's 30° mounting angle to minimize losses at entry.

The low-profile undertray maximizes air intake through the inlet while the Benzing diffuser profile reduces wake without compromising downforce. Multi-element side wings sit above the diffuser to add rear downforce while maintaining the car's overall center of pressure balance. This integrated layout formed the baseline for the dimensional sweeps performed in CFD.

Results

The B23 baseline aerodynamic package produced a total downforce of 382–415N at a drag of 172–178N, broken down by component as follows: rear wing 157–164N, front wing 132–145N, side wings 31N, and undertray 27–31N. The side wing and undertray contributions — the areas targeted in the B24 dimensional sweeps — provided a meaningful share of total downforce while maintaining the diffuser flow characteristics identified as favorable in the Zhang reference study. This baseline served as the starting point for the B24 iteration work.
