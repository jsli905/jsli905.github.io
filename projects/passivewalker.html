Passive Dynamic Walking Robot

Designed and prototyped a gravity-driven biped walker using dynamic modeling, simulation, and experimental validation

This project explores passive dynamic locomotion—walking without motors or active control. The system relies purely on gravity, inertia, and mechanical design to produce stable motion down a shallow incline. I modeled the walker as a multi-link pendulum system, simulated its gait behavior, and built a physical prototype to evaluate real-world performance. The project highlights the challenges of translating ideal dynamic models into physical systems, particularly in managing energy losses and stability.
Project Partners: David Bui, Ki Jung Park, Daniel Richards

Background

Passive dynamic walkers generate motion without actuation, using gravity to drive a natural gait cycle. These systems are often modeled as inverted pendulums and exhibit highly efficient motion, but are extremely sensitive to physical parameters such as mass distribution, slope angle, and joint constraints.

This project builds on classical models such as the rimless wheel and compass gait walker, extending them to a three-link system with knees. The goal was to achieve a stable walking cycle by tuning physical parameters and validating performance through both simulation and prototyping.

Modeling & Simulation

The walker was modeled as a three-link system using Lagrangian dynamics. The motion consists of two phases:

  • Swing phase: leg swings forward with an unlocked knee
  • Stance phase: leg supports the body with a locked knee

The system exhibits hybrid dynamics, combining continuous motion with discrete events such as heel strike. A Simulink model was developed to solve for joint angles and simulate gait cycles under varying conditions.

Parameter sweeps were conducted to identify stable walking behavior. The optimal configuration was found near a ramp angle of approximately 3°, where the walker maintained forward motion without excessive instability.

Prototype Design

A physical prototype was built to validate simulation results and evaluate real-world performance. The design included adjustable mass distribution to tune the center of mass and improve stability.

  • Leg length: 6.06 in
  • Center of mass: 3 in from hip
  • Stride length: 1.25 in
  • Ramp angle: 3°

Results & Failure Analysis

The prototype successfully demonstrated passive walking behavior, but was only able to sustain motion for approximately three steps before failing.

Observed Issues

  • Rapid energy loss due to friction and impact
  • High sensitivity to initial conditions
  • Knee buckling during stance phase
  • Insufficient stability to maintain continuous gait

While the simulation predicted stable motion, real-world effects such as joint compliance, imperfect impacts, and structural limitations significantly affected performance. This highlighted the gap between idealized dynamic models and physical implementation.

Redesign: Assisted Passive Walker

To address instability and knee buckling, a redesigned mechanism was developed incorporating minimal actuation while preserving passive dynamics.

  • Actuated knee joints for improved support
  • Cam system for timing knee motion
  • Pulley system for force transmission
  • “Flopper” mechanism for passive leg return

This approach introduces controlled assistance to improve robustness while maintaining the efficiency benefits of passive walking.
