Morphing Matter Structure

Designed, analyzed, and experimentally tested a deformable structure using mechanics of materials and structural testing

This project focused on designing a structure that could deform in a controlled way while still maintaining structural integrity. The goal was to apply mechanics of materials concepts to a real physical system and validate the design through calculations and experimental testing. Rather than being purely theoretical, the project required building a physical prototype, estimating stresses and deformation, calculating safety factors, and comparing experimental behavior to theoretical predictions. The project emphasized the full engineering workflow: design → build → test → analyze.

Course: Design of Morphing Materials and Mechanisms
Project Partners: Tyler Ho, Yuka Iwashita, Ryan Lee

Background

Many mechanical systems require structures that are both flexible and strong. Designing a structure that can deform predictably without failing requires an understanding of stress, strain, and load paths. The goal of this project was to design a structure that could undergo controlled deformation while maintaining structural integrity under load.

Instead of relying only on theory, the structure had to be physically built and tested. This required estimating the loads applied to the structure, calculating expected deformation, and determining whether the structure would fail or remain within a safe operating range.

Design

The structure was designed using mechanics of materials concepts such as bending stress, normal stress, and deformation. The goal was to create a structure that would visibly deform under load but not fail. This required balancing stiffness and flexibility while also ensuring that stress levels remained below material limits.

Load cases were estimated based on how the structure would be tested, and stress calculations were performed to determine whether the design would remain within a safe range. Safety factors were calculated to ensure the structure could be tested without risk of catastrophic failure.

Analysis and Calculations

Analytical calculations were performed to estimate stress and deformation under load. These calculations included estimating bending stress, normal stress, and expected deflection. Safety factors were then calculated to verify that the structure could safely undergo deformation without permanent damage.

The analytical results were later compared to experimental results in order to validate the design and determine whether the theoretical assumptions were accurate.

Manufacturing and Testing

After the design was finalized, a physical prototype was built and tested. A testing setup was created to apply loads and measure deformation. The goal was to determine whether the real structure behaved the same way as the analytical predictions.

Experimental results were compared to theoretical calculations in order to evaluate the accuracy of the model and identify potential sources of error such as material assumptions, boundary conditions, and manufacturing tolerances.

Results

The structure successfully demonstrated controlled deformation while maintaining structural integrity. Experimental results were consistent with analytical predictions, confirming that the design approach was valid. The project demonstrated how theoretical mechanics concepts can be applied to a real physical system and validated through testing.

The project also highlighted the importance of assumptions in engineering calculations and how experimental testing can be used to verify theoretical models.
