Passive Dynamic Walking Robot

Designed and prototyped a gravity-driven biped walker using dynamic modeling, simulation, and experimental validation

This project explores passive dynamic locomotion—walking without motors or active control. The system relies purely on gravity, inertia, and mechanical design to produce stable motion down a shallow incline. We modeled the walker as a multi-link pendulum system with continuous swing dynamics and discrete impact events, simulated its gait behavior, and built physical prototypes to evaluate real-world performance and iterate. The goal is to identify parameter regimes that produce stable, periodic walking (limit cycles) using only gravity and mechanical design. The project highlights the challenges of translating ideal dynamic models into physical systems, particularly in managing energy losses and stability.
Project Partners: David Bui, Ki Jung Park, Daniel Richards

Background

Passive dynamic walkers generate motion without actuation, using gravity to drive a natural gait cycle. These systems are often modeled as inverted pendulums and exhibit highly efficient motion, but are extremely sensitive to physical parameters such as mass distribution, slope angle, and joint constraints.

This project builds on classical models such as the rimless wheel and compass gait walker, extending them to a three-link system with knees. The goal was to achieve a stable walking cycle by tuning physical parameters and validating performance through both simulation and prototyping.

Our ultimate aim is to enable stable, controlled downhill walking at steeper inclines, which has been proven challenging for conventional passive bipeds. At higher inclinations, existing passive walkers often struggle to maintain an upright posture. To address this, we adjusted the mass distribution to position the center of mass near the central axle and introduced supplemental masses throughout the leg assembly to enhance dynamic stability.

Our efforts focused on refining design parameters through simulation-based analysis, guiding us with an understanding of integrating the desired hardware development for our product. A first generation prototype was designed and manufactured for testing, forming the foundation for ongoing improvements and modifications in simulation-based testing. We applied observations in the simulation in the real life prototype, which were also designed to test and validate our simulations. Our simulation progress allowed us to find out the reasons for our errors and achievements between our prototype iterations. Through validation in our simulation and real world tests, we were able to confidently make our prototype, based on our fundamental equations and simulation results.

Modeling

System Model

The simplest theoretical model of a passive walker device is that of the rimless wheel

The walker is modeled as a three-link system (a stance leg, a swing leg, and a torso) using generalized coordinates defined by joint angles. This model consists of a single point mass at the hip and straight legs radiating out of essentially a rimless wheel, and has a given analytical solution for its step-to-step behavior. By just having two of the legs and placing a pin between them, the legs may move relative to each other and the compass gait can be created, as seen below.

Lagrangian Equation

The dynamics are derived using Lagrangian mechanics.

$$ H(q)\ddot{q} + B(q,\dot{q})\dot{q} + G(q) = 0 $$

Inertia matrix, velocity-dependent (Coriolis/centrifugal), and gravitational terms for the three-link pendulum.

Unlocked Knees

Upon kneestrike, the knee will remain locked. The matrices below describe the system when the knee joint is free (unlocked), allowing three independent degrees of freedom:

Inertia matrix:

$$ H = \begin{bmatrix} H_{11} & H_{12} & H_{13} \\ H_{12} & H_{22} & H_{23} \\ H_{13} & H_{23} & H_{33} \end{bmatrix} $$

Coriolis and Centrifugal forces with varying joint velocities:

$$ B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & h_{122}\dot{q}_2 & h_{133}\dot{q}_3 \\ h_{211}\dot{q}_1 & 0 & h_{233}\dot{q}_3 \\ h_{311}\dot{q}_1 & h_{322}\dot{q}_2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Gravity Matrix

$$ G = \begin{bmatrix} -(m_s a_1 + m_t(l_s + a_2) + (m_h + m_s + m_t)L)g\sin(q_1) \\ (m_t b_2 + m_s l_t)g\sin(q_2) \\ m_s b_1 g \sin(q_3) \end{bmatrix} $$

Unlocked knee conditions:

$$ H_{11} = m_s a_1^2 + m_t(l_s + a_2)^2 + (m_h + m_s + m_t)L^2 $$ $$ H_{12} = -(m_t b_2 + m_s l_t)L \cos(q_2 - q_1) $$ $$ H_{13} = -m_s b_1 L \cos(q_3 - q_1) $$ $$ H_{22} = m_t b_2^2 + m_s l_t^2 $$ $$ H_{23} = m_s l_t b_1 \cos(q_3 - q_2) $$ $$ H_{33} = m_s b_1^2 $$
$$ h_{122} = -(m_t b_2 + m_s l_t)L \sin(q_1 - q_2) $$ $$ h_{133} = -m_s b_1 L \sin(q_1 - q_3) $$ $$ h_{211} = -h_{122}, \quad h_{311} = -h_{133} $$ $$ h_{233} = m_s l_t b_1 \sin(q_3 - q_2), \quad h_{322} = -h_{233} $$

Locked Knees

The system is re-derived for locked knees using a reduced Lagrangian formulation:

$$ H = \begin{bmatrix} H_{11} & H_{12} \\ H_{12} & H_{22} \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & h\dot{q}_2 \\ -h\dot{q}_1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ G = \begin{bmatrix} -(m_s a_1 + m_t(l_s + a_2) + (m_h + m_t + m_s)L)g\sin(q_1) \\ (m_t b_2 + m_s(l_t + b_1))g\sin(q_2) \end{bmatrix} $$

These equations govern swing foot impact, heel strike collision, and switching between stance and swing legs:

$$ H_{11} = m_s a_1^2 + m_t(l_s + a_2)^2 + (m_h + m_s + m_t)L^2 $$ $$ H_{12} = -(m_t b_2 + m_s(l_t + b_1))L\cos(q_2 - q_1) $$ $$ H_{22} = m_t b_2^2 + m_s(l_t + b_1)^2 $$ $$ h = -(m_t b_2 + m_s(l_t + b_1))L\sin(q_1 - q_2) $$

Simulations

Slope Angle Optimization

Using MATLAB and Simulink simulations, the optimal slope angle for the passive walking robot was determined to be 3°. This result was obtained by sweeping slope angles from 0° to 5° and evaluating gait performance using conditional logic.

Evaluation Method:

  • Parameter sweep: θ ∈ [0°, 5°]
  • Simulation-based validation
  • Stability determined via if-else logic conditions

A stable gait was defined as a periodic walking cycle without excessive or insufficient swing motion.

Stability Criteria

  • Periodic Gait Cycle: Joint states return to initial conditions after each step.
  • Kneestrike Dynamics: Swing leg fully extends and locks during impact.
  • Heelstrike Dynamics: Proper ground contact angle and momentum transfer.
  • Swing Leg Motion: Sufficient forward motion without instability or stalling.

Results

  • θ < 3°: Insufficient swing due to low gravitational potential energy.
  • θ > 5°: Excessive swing → instability, loss of balance, high-speed gait.
  • θ = 3°: Optimal — stable, repeatable periodic gait satisfying all conditions.

The governing equations were derived from the Lagrangian formulation, assuming uniform mass distribution along each leg segment.

3D Simulink / Simscape Model

A 3D dynamic model was developed using Simscape to represent a biped robot with a knee joint.

Model Design

  • Initial condition: robot collapses due to flexed knee configuration
  • Spring added between femur and tibia to restore extension
  • Prismatic rod mechanism used to simulate knee locking at heel strike

Key Challenges

  • Spring stiffness too high → prevented proper kneestrike
  • Contact dynamics introduced friction and bouncing
  • High sensitivity due to 3D complexity (many degrees of freedom)

While a partial gait cycle (single swing/stance phase) was achieved, instability from contact dynamics limited full-cycle performance.

Due to these challenges, the analysis transitioned to a 2D dynamic model for better control and interpretability.

2D Simulink Dynamics Simulation

A 2D simulation framework was developed in Simulink to analyze system dynamics and validate the derived equations.

Model Setup

  • Two dynamic phases: Stance and Swing
  • State vector: q = (q₁, q₂, q₃)

Initial Conditions:

q₁ = 15°, q₂ = 25°, q₃ = 0°
q̇₁ = 0°/s, q̇₂ = 0°/s, q̇₃ = 0°/s

Model Parameters

  • Tibia length and mass = 1/5 of femur
  • Hip, knee, and foot trajectories computed via transformation matrices

Results & Observations

  • Stance phase (q₁): Physically reasonable behavior
  • Swing phase issue: q₂ increased positively instead of decreasing
  • This caused instability in q₃ and overall system divergence

Critical Issue:

Expected: q₂ → negative during swing
Observed: q₂ → increasing positive values

Despite testing multiple initial conditions, no configuration produced the expected negative evolution of q₂. Resolving this behavior was identified as the primary requirement for achieving a stable simulated gait.

Prototyping

We validated simulation results and iterated on our simulation by evaluating real-world performance through various test prototypes. These designs included adjustable mass distribution to tune the center of mass and improve stability. We then used the final simulation results as a jumping off point for our final physical prototype.

  • Leg length: 6.06 in
  • Center of mass: 3 in from hip
  • Stride length: 1.25 in
  • Ramp angle: 3°

While simulations predicted stable limit cycle behavior, the physical prototype exhibited rapid energy loss. The prototype achieved a maximum of three steps before failure, highlighting the sensitivity of passive dynamic systems to real-world imperfections.

Using what we learned, we updated our simulation and designed a kneed prototype.

Key Sources of Discrepancy

  • Joint friction and bearing losses
  • Non-ideal (inelastic) impacts
  • Structural compliance
  • Knee buckling under load

To address instability and knee buckling in this prototype, a redesigned mechanism was developed with knee joints controlled by a pulley and cam system. This incorporated minimal actuation while preserving passive dynamics.

  • Actuated knee joints for improved support
  • Cam system for timing knee motion
  • Pulley system for force transmission
  • “Flopper” mechanism for passive leg return

This approach introduces controlled assistance to improve robustness while maintaining the efficiency benefits of passive walking.

Results & Failure Analysis

While the simulation predicted stable motion, real-world effects such as joint compliance, imperfect impacts, and structural limitations significantly affected performance. This highlighted the gap between idealized dynamic models and physical implementation.
