Passive Dynamic Walking Robot

Designed and prototyped a gravity-driven biped walker using dynamic modeling, simulation, and experimental validation

This project explores passive dynamic locomotion—walking without motors or active control. The system relies purely on gravity, inertia, and mechanical design to produce stable motion down a shallow incline. We modeled the walker as a multi-link pendulum system with continuous swing dynamics and discrete impact events, simulated its gait behavior, and built physical prototypes to evaluate real-world performance and iterate. The goal is to identify parameter regimes that produce stable, periodic walking (limit cycles) using only gravity and mechanical design. The project highlights the challenges of translating ideal dynamic models into physical systems, particularly in managing energy losses and stability.
Project Partners: David Bui, Ki Jung Park, Daniel Richards

Background

Passive dynamic walkers generate motion without actuation, using gravity to drive a natural gait cycle. These systems are often modeled as inverted pendulums and exhibit highly efficient motion, but are extremely sensitive to physical parameters such as mass distribution, slope angle, and joint constraints.

This project builds on classical models such as the rimless wheel and compass gait walker, extending them to a three-link system with knees. The goal was to achieve a stable walking cycle by tuning physical parameters and validating performance through both simulation and prototyping.

Modeling

System Model

The walker is modeled as a three-link system (a stance leg, a swing leg, and a torso) using generalized coordinates defined by joint angles. The dynamics are derived using Lagrangian mechanics.

Generalized coordinates: θ = [θ₁, θ₂, θ₃]

The equations of motion take the standard manipulator form:

:contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}
  • M(θ): configuration-dependent inertia matrix
  • C(θ, θ̇): Coriolis and centrifugal effects
  • G(θ): gravitational forces driving motion

These nonlinear coupled differential equations govern the continuous motion of the walker during the swing phase.

Hybrid Gait Dynamics

The walking cycle is modeled as a hybrid dynamical system consisting of continuous dynamics and discrete transitions.

Continuous Phase (Swing)

:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

During the swing phase, the system evolves according to the nonlinear equations of motion derived from the Lagrangian.

Discrete Phase (Heel Strike Impact)

:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

At heel strike, an instantaneous, inelastic collision occurs. The system undergoes a discrete state transition where velocities change abruptly due to impact constraints.

  • Angular velocities are reset via conservation of angular momentum
  • Energy is dissipated at each step
  • Leg roles are swapped (stance ↔ swing)

This hybrid structure makes the system highly sensitive to parameters and initial conditions.

Gait Stability & Limit Cycles

Stable walking corresponds to a periodic solution (limit cycle) of the hybrid system, where the state repeats after each step.

:contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

A Poincaré map is used to analyze step-to-step behavior. A stable gait satisfies:

  • Existence of a fixed point: x* = P(x*)
  • Eigenvalues of the linearized map lie within the unit circle

Stability depends strongly on system parameters such as slope angle, mass distribution, and leg geometry.

Simulation & Numerical Methods

The system was simulated in MATLAB/Simulink using numerical ODE solvers with event detection to capture heel strike conditions.

  • ODE integration for continuous dynamics
  • Event detection for ground contact
  • State reset using impact map
  • Parameter sweeps for stability analysis

Simulations identified a narrow range of parameters that produced stable walking behavior, particularly near a ramp angle of ~3°.

Prototyping

We validated simulation results and iterated on our simulation and evaluate real-world performance through various test prototypes. These designs included adjustable mass distribution to tune the center of mass and improve stability. We then used the final simulation results as a jumping off point for our final physical prototype.

While simulations predicted stable limit cycle behavior, the physical prototype exhibited rapid energy loss and instability.

  • Leg length: 6.06 in
  • Center of mass: 3 in from hip
  • Stride length: 1.25 in
  • Ramp angle: 3°

Key Sources of Discrepancy

  • Joint friction and bearing losses
  • Non-ideal (inelastic) impacts
  • Structural compliance
  • Knee buckling under load

The prototype achieved a maximum of three steps before failure, highlighting the sensitivity of passive dynamic systems to real-world imperfections.

To address instability and knee buckling, a redesigned mechanism was developed with knee joints controlled by a pulley and cam system. This incorporated minimal actuation while preserving passive dynamics.

  • Actuated knee joints for improved support
  • Cam system for timing knee motion
  • Pulley system for force transmission
  • “Flopper” mechanism for passive leg return

This approach introduces controlled assistance to improve robustness while maintaining the efficiency benefits of passive walking.

Results & Failure Analysis

The prototype successfully demonstrated passive walking behavior, but was only able to sustain motion for approximately three steps before failing.

Observed Issues

  • Rapid energy loss due to friction and impact
  • High sensitivity to initial conditions
  • Knee buckling during stance phase
  • Insufficient stability to maintain continuous gait

While the simulation predicted stable motion, real-world effects such as joint compliance, imperfect impacts, and structural limitations significantly affected performance. This highlighted the gap between idealized dynamic models and physical implementation.
