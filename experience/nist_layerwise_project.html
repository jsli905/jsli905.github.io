National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Layerwise Imaging Projec

Additive manufacturing offers transformative potential for defense, aerospace, and medical part production — but adoption is bottlenecked by the complete absence of public qualification standards. Without qualification, each organization must conduct its own costly, time-consuming testing programs, and data almost always remains proprietary.

  • No public qualification framework exists for AM processes or materials in aerospace/defense
  • Internal defects (gas pores, lack-of-fusion, cracking, warping) occur during L-PBF and can cause part failure if undetected
  • Layerwise images captured by the AMMT's in-situ camera came out rotated and perspective-distorted due to a 63.2° off-axis camera angle — unusable for accurate defect localization without correction
  • The AM Material Database (ammd.nist.gov) required manual XML curation of experimental data — a slow, error-prone process not scalable to the volume of tests needed for qualification
  • Existing test data from other labs remained siloed; a shared, structured public database was needed to pool results toward qualification

Additive manufacturing generates large amounts of process data that can be used to improve material characterization, process understanding, and defect detection. During my time at NIST, I developed software tools to automate data handling, process experimental data, and analyze manufacturing images collected during laser powder bed fusion builds.

Role Summary

Developed a computer vision pipeline to correct perspective-distorted layerwise images (LWIs) captured by the AMMT's angled in-situ camera during laser powder-bed fusion (L-PBF) builds. The corrected images enable accurate real-world localization of surface defects for future closed-loop feedback to the AMMT during printing.

Objective

In laser powder-bed fusion, defects form layer by layer and are quickly buried under the next powder coat. Capturing an image of each layer before and after laser scanning allows us to observe print behavior and predict defects while the print is happening The AMMT's layer imaging camera provides this capability, but introduced aome problems:

  • The camera was mounted at 63.2° from surface normal with viewing vector <0.45, 0, 0.89>, distorting all images with perspective foreshortening
  • Images were also captured upside-down, requiring rotation before any processing
  • Without correction, defect pixel coordinates in the LWI could not be mapped to real-world build-plate coordinates — making the images useless for accurate defect localization
  • Existing code had bugs in the undistortion routine; no systematic comparison of methods had been done
  • Low contrast between metal parts and surrounding powder background made automated edge detection unreliable, requiring a hybrid approach using build command data for ground truth

If defects cannot be accurately located in real-world coordinates, the AMMT cannot adjust laser parameters in real time or stop an unrecoverable print.

Requirements & Constraints

  • Accuracy: Undistorted images must map pixel coordinates to real-world build-plate coordinates with sufficient precision to localize defects for closed-loop AMMT feedback
  • Ground truth: A synthetic image generated from XYPT build command data (laser x/y at 10 µs resolution) served as the ideal reference — methods were evaluated against this
  • Resolution: LWIs were 342 × 508 px; the pipeline needed to upsample to 684 × 1016 px so undistortion preserved resolution rather than degrading it
  • Camera constraints: Fixed 63.2° off-axis angle — perspective distortion was intrinsic to the setup and could not be changed; correction had to be done in software
  • Image quality constraints: Low part-to-background contrast prevented fully automated corner detection; point selection required manual assistance in Experiment 1
  • Generalizability: The pipeline needed to work for both 12-part and 4-part build layouts across two different experiments with different illumination setups
  • Experiment 1 illumination: Two LED bars at 850 nm, brightfield; camera viewed through plexiglass (with partial obstruction of Parts 1 & 2)
  • Experiment 2 illumination: Three directional LEDs at 470 nm (one diffuse brick LED, two linear arrays); no acrylic obstruction — three images per state (A and B) per layer

Results & Impact

The automation pipeline for XML curation eliminated manual data preparation steps, enabling faster and more reliable contributions to the publicly accessible AM Material Database. The layerwise image pipeline produced corrected, cropped, and organized image datasets ready for downstream machine learning and defect classification work. Defect pattern findings (elevated edges, corner protrusion, subsurface blobs) gave the AMMT team actionable hypotheses for parameter adjustment and future automated image analysis and in-situ defect corretion work.
