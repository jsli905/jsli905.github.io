Main Project: Automated Stator Teardown

Background & Goal

Scrapped stators, ones which fail during manufacturing or fail quality inspections were mostly being discarded entirely, including their laminated stator stacks, which are expensive components. There is a manual process for tearing down stators, which would take an hour per stator and caused wrist injuries. The goal was to create an automated process to tear down scrapped stators and recover the stator stacks for reuse, replacing the labor-intensive manual process.

Process Overview

The automated teardown process consists of five sequential stages:

1. Cut the endturn off the scrapped stator in a CNC mill
2. Operator moves the cut stator to the copper pull machine
3. Copper wire removed from the stator stack and dropped into copper recycle bucket
4. Stack moves to the clean machine
5. Clean machine clears all cooling channels of copper dust

Benefits of the automated system vs. prior manual process:

  • 93% reduction in required headcount
  • 89% reduction in process time
  • Enables reuse of stacks from failed stators — significant material cost savings

CNC Mill: Endturn Cutting

Setup & Tooling

I set up the CNC machine, designed fixturing to hold stators in place during cutting, and sourced and attached the cutting tooling. The fixture uses a combination of a fixed mount and a pneumatic-cylinder-actuated clamp to secure the stator. Additional setup elements included:

  • Fan for chip and dust management
  • Two cutting disc sizes to handle different stator variants
  • Mesh filter to prevent copper dust from clogging the vacuum
  • 3D-printed vacuum adapter to connect the fixture to the CNC machine's vacuum system

G-Code Program

I learned to write G-code, tested multiple programs, and finalized a cutting program. The spindle rotates clockwise while the path moves counterclockwise, which pushes copper dust toward the center rather than into the cooling channels. The program cuts the bottom (non-lead) endturn only.

Some past iterations of the teardown design I tested included using touch prob detects whether the stator was an induction motor (IM) or permanent magnet motor (PM) to cut the endturn. A PM motor is almost twice the height of an IM motor, and will have different endturn internal and external diameters. A key design decision was using a single program for all stator variants and only cut one endturn. I also considered having the operator select an IM or PM program to run (and wrote bot individual programs), however the potential consequences of operator error was too big.

  • Pro: Missing the endturn cutoff could break the spindle and damage the machine. Simpler and faster to operate. Would allow for copper pulling configuration (see next section)
  • Con: Shorter tool lifespan: The geometric window that works for both variants is also narrow (0.7 mm clearance from the IM interior and PM exterior), so the disc will need to be replaced when it is worn down to the point where it no longer cuts the entire PM enturn.

Cutting only the bottom endturn also avoids the safety hazard of the lead-side endturn flying off during the cut, and keeps cooling channels cleaner by reducing total copper dust generated.

Copper Dust: Main Roadblock

The primary challenge was preventing copper dust from entering the stator's cooling channels during the cut. This was resolved through the directed cutting motion (dust pushed inward toward the center), a cooling channel cover fitted during the cut, and the downstream clean machine that clears any remaining dust.

Copper Pull Machine

Pushing vs. Pulling

The manual process had production associates physically pushing copper wire out of the stator slots. I evaluated whether to automate a push or pull approach, starting with napkin-math buckling calculations on a push scenario. The analysis drove the decision to pull:

  • Reduced CNC cycle time: a two-endturn-cut program ran more than twice as long, so keeping to a single cut simplified the upstream step significantly
  • Eleminates risk of wire buckling and machine component failure
  • Reduced risk of stator stack (created from layers of sheet metal) delamination
  • Avoids safety and cooling channel cleanliness issues associated with cutting the top endturn

Force Testing

To spec the pneumatic cylinder, I needed to know the actual force required to pull copper wire from the stator slots. I ran Instron pull tests with the stator stack fixed in place to measure extraction force. Key findings:

  • Impregnated (impreg) stators cannot be processed. The resin bonding prevents clean wire removal
  • Pre-impreg stators cannot be cut into sections before pulling without damaging the windings. The design must pull the full endturn intact
  • Force data was used directly to determine pneumatic cylinder specifications

Machine Design & Validation

The machine operates across five stages: load cut stator -> move and raise stator -> pull copper -> return stack to loading position -> grippers release copper into disposal below the conveyance. The ID clamp engages via a keyway-aligned twist-and-lock mechanism; a PM sensor on the pallet lift detects whether the incoming part is an IM or PM stator and sets the pneumatic cylinder extension accordingly.

Specific gripper and pulling mechanism details are under NDA. The design uses gripping fingers with springs to engage the endturns, and a pneumatic cylinder provides the extraction force. I ran multiple FEA simulations and fatigue analyses to validate the structural design.

Other Projects

Pre-TUF1 Alignment Jig

The number one fault on TUF1 machines across all three wind lines was caused by operator misplacement of barrel connectors at the previous stations. I designed a jig that physically locates the correct placement, eliminating the dependency on operator judgment for a precision positioning task.

  • Fork positions adjustable in X, Y, and Z to calibrate to the correct location
  • Operator pulls the top platform toward them; a laser sensor confirms the platform has reached the stopper
  • Connector dropped directly onto the forks at the correct position
  • Created an ergonomic handle, getting feedback from production associates, to eliminate operator fatigue as much as possible

Vibration-Based Impreg Machine Fault Detection

I investigated using vibration sensors and Fast Fourier Transforms (FFTs) to detect developing faults in the impregnation machine. The frequency domain is more effective than time-domain data for multi-frequency vibration analysis, enabling identification of characteristic fault signatures:

  • Shaft balance issues: large radial variation and marked amplitude increase in the FFT
  • Misalignment: amplitude increase at specific harmonic frequencies
  • Looseness: increase in number of harmonics or rise in fundamental frequency amplitude

Post-SIT Sensors

Lugs on stators were being crushed at the post-SIT station, likely due to parts arriving at the wrong angle. I designed and fabricated sensor mounts, installed and wired the sensors on the line, set sensor parameters, wrote the PLC logic, pushed the logic, and monitored the lines after deployment.

Lessons Learned

  • The best projects come from observing and looking for areas of improvement proactively: the stator teardown project was one that came out of looking for areas to improve on when I wanted more projects. All my other projects came from actively asking around to find where help was needed, rather than waiting to be assigned work. Walking the line, talking to other engineers and production associates, and paying attention kept me busy and learning
  • Adapt fast when structure changes around you: the internship involved several management changes, and my assigned mentor became a manager the day before I onboarded (so I started without one). Learning to navigate ambiguity, figure out who to go to for what, be resourceful, and stay productive without a consistent point of contact was an unexpected but valuable experience.
  • Large-scale manufacturing is a different world: having never worked in a high-volume factory before, the pace, coordination complexity, and real stakes of changes on active production lines were all larger than expected
  • Getting production downtime is a genuine constraint: scheduling time on a live line to install or test something turned out to be one of the biggest practical blockers. It requires advance planning and cross-team coordination in a way that isn't obvious from an engineering desk, and I'd build it into project timelines more explicitly next time
  • Cross-functional coordination is as much of the job as the engineering: getting anything onto the line required alignment with production associates, process engineers, the quality team, and external part manufacturers. Communicating progress clearly and proactively across all of them was essential
  • Monitor changes after deployment: pushing a logic change or hardware modification and walking away isn't enough. The first hours of operation after a change often surface issues, and being present to observe and respond is part of the job
  • Tolerances need to be triple-checked before parts go out: rushing a few designs led to missing clearance tolerances and required me personally modifying parts
  • Outsourced machining lead times are long: parts that needed external machining took far longer than initially assumed. Planning fabrication timelines backward from the actual need date, with realistic lead time buffers, is essential at scale
  • Small changes on a production line can have large effects in both directions: a seemingly minor modification can ripple into downstream faults or, conversely, eliminate a recurring issue entirely. That asymmetry demands caution and thorough communication before anything goes live
  • Skills are learnable on the job: G-code, FANUC robots, and PLCs were not part of my background coming in. These were all skills I picked up during the internship
  • Range of skills required at a manufacturing company: the breadth of what came up (mechanical design, FEA, G-code, PLC programming, SQL queries, vibration analysis) was broader than expected for a single internship, and learning to context-switch between them quickly was a skill in itself
